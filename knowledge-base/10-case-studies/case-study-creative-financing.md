# Case Study: Turning a Dead Deal into $16,799 Using Subject-To Creative Financing

## Quick Reference

- **Property**: 3-bed/2-bath single-family home, ARV $285,000
- **Challenge**: Seller owed $248,201 (mortgage + arrears), traditional wholesale MAO was only $174,500
- **Solution**: Subject-to acquisition with assignment to buy-and-hold investor
- **Wholesaler Investment**: $8,201 (arrears + moving assistance)
- **Wholesaler Profit**: $16,799 net after all costs
- **Timeline**: 23 days from initial contact to closing
- **Key Insight**: The existing 3.25% mortgage became the deal's most valuable asset
- **Buyer Benefit**: $25,000 instant equity + $883/month cash flow
- **Seller Outcome**: Avoided foreclosure, received $5,000 moving money, protected credit
- **Risk Mitigation**: Used proper authorization forms, title insurance, and buyer qualification

## The Property and Seller Situation

### Property Details

The subject property was a 1,987 square foot single-family home built in 2019 in a growing suburban neighborhood. Here's what the numbers looked like:

| Metric | Amount |
|--------|--------|
| After Repair Value (ARV) | $285,000 |
| Existing Mortgage Balance | $245,000 |
| Interest Rate | 3.25% (30-year fixed) |
| Monthly PITI Payment | $1,067 |
| Months Behind | 3 |
| Total Arrears | $3,201 |
| Estimated Repairs | $15,000 |
| Market Rent | $1,950/month |

### The Seller's Story

Marcus and Denise Thompson had purchased their home in 2021 during the historic low-interest-rate period. They locked in a 3.25% rate—a rate that would be nearly impossible to obtain in today's market. However, Marcus received an unexpected job transfer to another state with only 30 days' notice.

The move expenses, combined with carrying two households temporarily, caused them to fall behind on payments. By the time they contacted me, they were three months delinquent and had received a notice of default. Their situation was urgent:

- **Credit score dropping** due to missed payments
- **New employer** requiring relocation within 45 days
- **No savings** remaining after moving expenses
- **Emotional exhaustion** from managing a cross-country move while facing foreclosure

## Why Traditional Wholesale Didn't Work

My first instinct was to run the standard wholesale formula:

```
Maximum Allowable Offer (MAO) = ARV × 70% - Repairs - Wholesale Fee
MAO = $285,000 × 0.70 - $15,000 - $10,000
MAO = $199,500 - $15,000 - $10,000
MAO = $174,500
```

The problem was immediately apparent. The sellers owed $248,201 (mortgage balance plus arrears). Even if I paid zero for my wholesale fee, I'd need to offer $73,701 more than any cash buyer would pay.

If I walked away, here's what happened to everyone:
- **Sellers**: Foreclosure, credit destruction, potential deficiency judgment
- **Me**: No deal, no profit
- **End buyer**: Never knows about opportunity

Traditional wholesale was mathematically impossible. This deal was "dead" by conventional standards.

## The Creative Structure: Subject-To With Assignment

Instead of buying the property outright and assigning the purchase contract, I structured a subject-to acquisition that I would then assign to an end buyer.

### What "Subject-To" Means

A subject-to transaction means acquiring the property "subject to" the existing financing remaining in place. The mortgage stays in the seller's name, but ownership transfers to the buyer, who takes over making payments.

### The Deal Structure

**Step 1: Agreement with Sellers**
- I would take title to the property subject to their existing mortgage
- I would cure the $3,201 arrears to stop foreclosure proceedings
- I would provide $5,000 cash for moving expenses
- They would deed the property to my entity (or my assignee)

**Step 2: Assignment to End Buyer**
- I marketed the deal to my investor buyer list
- Found a buy-and-hold investor interested in cash-flowing rentals
- Assigned my position for a $25,000 assignment fee

**Step 3: End Buyer's Acquisition**
The end buyer received:
- Property with ARV of $285,000
- Existing 3.25% financing (worth its weight in gold in today's rate environment)
- Monthly payment of only $1,067 PITI
- Market rent of $1,950 = **$883/month positive cash flow**
- All-in cost of approximately $260,000 (mortgage + repairs)
- **Instant equity of $25,000**

## Presenting the Creative Solution

### Initial Conversation

I didn't lead with "subject-to." Instead, I focused on their problems first:

> "Marcus, it sounds like the priority is stopping this foreclosure, protecting your credit as much as possible, and getting some cash to help with your move. If I could solve all three problems while taking this property completely off your plate, would that be worth exploring?"

Once they agreed, I explained the mechanics:

> "Here's how this works. I'm going to take over responsibility for this house and its payments. Your mortgage will technically stay in your name until we refinance or sell it down the road, but you won't make another payment. I'll bring your loan current, give you $5,000 for moving expenses, and you walk away free and clear. The foreclosure stops, your credit starts recovering, and you focus on your new life."

### Why This Pitch Worked

1. **Problem-focused**: I addressed their pain points before my solution
2. **Simple language**: No jargon, no confusion
3. **Benefit-stacked**: Listed every advantage they'd receive
4. **Risk addressed**: Acknowledged the mortgage staying in their name proactively

## Seller Concerns and How They Were Addressed

### Concern #1: "What if you stop making payments?"

**My Response**: "That's a fair concern. Here's what we'll do: First, I'm signing a legally binding agreement to make these payments. Second, I'm putting an investor into this property who has significant cash reserves and a track record of owning rentals. Third, I'll give you quarterly updates for the first year showing the payments are current. If anything ever goes wrong, you have legal recourse against me."

**Documentation**: I provided a payment performance agreement and contact information for the end buyer (with their permission).

### Concern #2: "Could this hurt my credit?"

**My Response**: "Right now, your credit is being hurt every month this goes unpaid. Once I bring it current, those late payments stop. The mortgage will still appear on your credit report, but it will show as current and paid on time. Many of my sellers have gone on to buy new homes within 18-24 months."

### Concern #3: "What about the due-on-sale clause?"

**My Response**: "You're right that most mortgages have a due-on-sale clause that theoretically lets the bank call the loan due when ownership transfers. In practice, banks rarely exercise this clause when payments are current because it costs them money to foreclose and they're earning interest. We've done dozens of these transactions without issue. However, I want you to understand this risk exists, which is why I'm putting an experienced investor in place who can refinance if needed."

## Legal and Title Considerations

### Documentation Required

1. **Purchase and Sale Agreement**: Standard contract with subject-to addendum
2. **Subject-To Addendum**: Specifically outlined existing financing terms, seller disclosures, and buyer responsibilities
3. **Authorization to Release Information**: Allowed me to communicate with the lender
4. **Warranty Deed**: Transferred ownership from sellers to end buyer
5. **Assignment of Contract**: Transferred my equitable interest to end buyer

### Title Insurance

We closed through a title company experienced with creative financing transactions. Key points:

- **Title search** confirmed no additional liens or judgments
- **Owner's title policy** issued to end buyer
- **Lender's policy** remained in place (existing mortgage)
- **Closing agent** collected arrears payoff figure directly from servicer

### Escrow Instructions

The title company held all funds in escrow with specific instructions:
- Arrears payment wired directly to loan servicer
- Moving assistance funds released to sellers at closing
- Assignment fee released to me after deed recording

## Buyer Requirements for Creative Deals

Not every buyer can handle a subject-to acquisition. Here's what I required:

### Financial Requirements
- Minimum $50,000 liquid reserves (verified via bank statements)
- Proof of income or other cash-flowing assets
- Ability to refinance within 24-36 months if necessary

### Experience Requirements
- Previous rental property ownership preferred
- Understanding of subject-to mechanics and risks
- Written acknowledgment of due-on-sale clause risk

### Operational Requirements
- Property management plan in place
- Local presence or management company identified
- Insurance policy naming them as insured with mortgagee clause intact

## Risk Mitigation Strategies Used

### Risk 1: Due-on-Sale Clause Acceleration
**Mitigation**: Placed qualified buyer with refinancing capability; used land trust structure to reduce lender visibility

### Risk 2: Buyer Default
**Mitigation**: Verified liquid reserves; obtained personal guarantee; maintained seller communication channel

### Risk 3: Title Issues
**Mitigation**: Full title search; owner's title insurance; closed through experienced creative-financing-friendly title company

### Risk 4: Seller Remorse
**Mitigation**: Provided cooling-off period; documented all disclosures; maintained written communication trail

### Risk 5: Insurance Gaps
**Mitigation**: Verified new policy in place before closing; confirmed mortgagee clause properly endorsed

## Key Lessons Learned

### Lesson 1: Dead Deals Aren't Always Dead
The traditional formula showed a $73,701 gap. Creative financing bridged that gap completely and created profit for everyone involved.

### Lesson 2: The Mortgage Is the Asset
That 3.25% interest rate was worth more than the equity. Today's buyers would pay a premium to access that rate, and they did.

### Lesson 3: Seller Problems > Property Numbers
Marcus and Denise didn't care about ARV calculations. They cared about stopping foreclosure, getting moving money, and moving forward with their lives. Solve the human problem first.

### Lesson 4: Buyer Qualification Is Non-Negotiable
Creative deals carry more risk. Placing an unqualified buyer could result in default, seller lawsuits, and reputation damage. Screen rigorously.

### Lesson 5: Documentation Protects Everyone
Every disclosure, every acknowledgment, every communication in writing. Creative financing attracts scrutiny—make sure your paperwork is bulletproof.

## When to Use Creative Structures

Creative financing becomes appropriate when:

- **Low or negative equity** makes traditional wholesale impossible
- **Seller motivation** is extremely high (foreclosure, divorce, relocation)
- **Existing financing** has favorable terms (low interest rate, assumable loan)
- **Seller needs relief** more than cash
- **Traditional sale** would net seller nothing after commissions and closing costs
- **Time pressure** prevents waiting for retail buyer

Creative financing is **not** appropriate when:

- Seller has significant equity and wants maximum cash
- You lack understanding of the structures and risks
- Local laws restrict subject-to or seller financing
- You don't have qualified buyers for creative deals
- Seller cannot be educated about the transaction

## Common Mistakes

1. **Presenting creative financing before building rapport**: Sellers need to trust you before agreeing to complex structures
2. **Failing to verify mortgage terms**: Always obtain a payoff statement and mortgage documents
3. **Skipping buyer qualification**: Unqualified buyers create liability for you and the seller
4. **Using title companies unfamiliar with creative deals**: Inexperienced closers kill these transactions
5. **Neglecting insurance coordination**: Coverage gaps create massive liability exposure
6. **Overpromising on due-on-sale risk**: Be honest about this risk; never guarantee it won't happen
7. **Forgetting ongoing seller communication**: Maintain contact to prevent seller panic
8. **Ignoring state-specific regulations**: Some states have specific requirements for subject-to transactions
