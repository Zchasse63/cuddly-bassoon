# Case Study: Navigating an Ethical Dilemma - When Profit Conflicts with Doing Right

## Quick Reference

- Ethical dilemmas in wholesaling often involve information asymmetry—you know more than the seller
- Short-term profit maximization can destroy long-term reputation and referral business
- The "newspaper test" and "grandmother test" help clarify ethical decisions
- Transparency typically generates more lifetime value than exploitation
- Document your reasoning process for accountability
- Fair deals create referrals; predatory deals create complaints and legal exposure
- Your reputation is your most valuable long-term asset in this business
- Ethical behavior compounds positively over time through referrals and trust
- Walking away from a bad deal is always an option
- Every interaction shapes your identity as a business professional

## The Property and Seller Situation

Margaret Thompson was a 72-year-old widow who had just inherited her late brother's property at 847 Oakwood Drive. The 3-bedroom, 2-bathroom ranch home sat on a quarter-acre lot in a working-class neighborhood experiencing steady appreciation. Her brother had lived there for 35 years but had let maintenance slide during his final years battling illness.

The property condition was rough but far from catastrophic. It needed approximately $40,000 in repairs: roof replacement ($12,000), HVAC system ($8,000), kitchen updates ($10,000), and cosmetic work throughout ($10,000). The neighborhood's comparable sales showed renovated homes selling for $340,000-$360,000.

Margaret lived 300 miles away, had no interest in managing repairs, and needed funds for her own retirement. She was overwhelmed, unfamiliar with real estate, and visibly stressed during our first meeting. Her brother had no mortgage, so the property was free and clear.

During the initial appointment, it became clear that Margaret had done almost no research. She mentioned a neighbor had offered her $150,000 cash, and she was considering accepting because "at least it would be done."

**Market Analysis:**
- As-is market value: $265,000-$280,000
- After-repair value (ARV): $350,000
- Repair costs: $40,000
- Typical investor purchase price: $210,000-$225,000

## The Ethical Dilemma Encountered

Here's where my conscience started wrestling with my profit motive.

Margaret believed the property was worth somewhere between $150,000 and $180,000. She had no concept of the actual market value. When I ran my numbers, I realized I could offer $175,000, still beat the neighbor's offer, and wholesale the contract to my investor buyer for $215,000—netting me a $40,000 assignment fee.

The math worked beautifully. Margaret would be happy because she'd get $25,000 more than the neighbor offered. My buyer would be happy with a solid deal. I'd make my largest assignment fee ever.

But I'd be taking advantage of her ignorance to the tune of approximately $75,000-$90,000 in equity she didn't know she had.

## Competing Interests: Profit vs. Doing Right

**The profit argument:**
- I found the deal through my marketing efforts and relationship building
- She was going to sell cheap anyway—why not to me?
- I'm running a business, not a charity
- Every buyer in this business seeks to purchase below market value
- She'd still walk away with more than the neighbor offered

**The ethical argument:**
- She didn't understand her options or the property's true value
- Her decision was based on incomplete information I could easily provide
- Taking this deal felt like taking advantage of a grieving, elderly woman
- I wouldn't want someone doing this to my grandmother
- This wasn't a sophisticated seller who understood the trade-offs

The tension was real. I could feel the pull of that $40,000 fee. That's mortgage payments for a year. That's marketing budget for six months. That's life-changing money for my young business.

## Options Considered

I spent three days wrestling with this decision and identified four paths forward:

1. **Proceed with the low offer** - Maximize profit, justify it as "business"
2. **Walk away entirely** - Remove myself from the situation
3. **Provide full transparency and offer fair wholesale price** - Educate Margaret on her options while still offering to help through wholesaling at a fair price
4. **Refer her to a traditional real estate agent** - Remove myself but ensure she gets professional guidance

## Decision-Making Process

I applied three tests that now form my ethical framework:

**The Newspaper Test:** Would I be comfortable if this transaction appeared on the front page of my local newspaper? Absolutely not. "Local investor pays elderly widow $175,000 for $280,000 property" is not a headline I wanted associated with my name.

**The Grandmother Test:** Would I do this deal if Margaret were my grandmother? The answer was immediate and visceral: no.

**The Long-Term Reputation Test:** In five years, will this deal help or hurt my business? Taking advantage of vulnerable sellers creates complaints, negative reviews, potential legal exposure, and a reputation that makes future deals harder.

## Action Taken and Rationale

I chose Option 3: full transparency with a fair wholesale offer.

At our second meeting, I brought comparable sales data showing recent as-is sales of $265,000-$280,000 in her neighborhood. I explained her options:

1. Sell to a traditional buyer through an agent (likely $260,000-$275,000 after sitting on market 60-90 days)
2. Sell to an investor like me at a discount for speed and convenience
3. Keep the property and hire a property manager to rent it ($1,800/month potential)

I then offered $240,000 cash, closing in 14 days, no inspections, no contingencies. This would give me room to assign at $255,000-$260,000 for a $15,000-$20,000 fee.

I explained: "Margaret, I want to be transparent. I make money by connecting motivated sellers with investors who can close quickly. I could have offered you $175,000 and you might have accepted. But that wouldn't be right, and I'd rather make a smaller profit on a deal we both feel good about."

## Consequences: Short-Term and Long-Term

**Short-term (0-30 days):**
- Closed the deal at $240,000
- Assigned to my investor buyer for $257,000
- Assignment fee: $17,000 (vs. potential $40,000)
- Immediate "loss" of $23,000 in potential profit

**Long-term (6-24 months):**
- Margaret referred her nephew who was divorcing and needed to sell quickly ($9,500 assignment fee)
- Margaret's church friend contacted me for an inherited property ($12,000 assignment fee)
- Margaret posted a glowing review on Google that I still use in marketing
- Her attorney began referring probate clients to me (3 deals over 18 months, $34,000 total fees)

**Total financial impact:** The ethical choice generated approximately $55,500 in additional revenue over 24 months versus $23,000 in "lost" profit—a net positive of $32,500.

## Reputation and Relationship Impact

The ripple effects extended beyond direct financial returns:

- Margaret became a vocal advocate who described me as "honest and trustworthy"
- Her attorney's referrals came with built-in credibility
- I developed a reputation as an ethical operator in a industry plagued by bad actors
- My confidence in my own business practices increased dramatically
- Seller appointments became easier because I felt genuinely proud of my approach

## How It Felt to Make the Right Choice

Walking away from $23,000 in immediate profit was physically uncomfortable. I second-guessed myself for weeks. But every time I saw Margaret's testimonial or received a referral from her network, that discomfort transformed into professional pride.

The hardest part wasn't the decision—it was trusting that doing the right thing would eventually pay off. It did, but even if it hadn't, I'd still have my integrity and self-respect intact.

## Key Lessons Learned

1. **Information asymmetry creates ethical obligation.** When you know more than the seller, you have a responsibility to ensure they're making informed decisions.

2. **Short-term thinking is expensive.** The $23,000 I "left on the table" returned more than double through referrals.

3. **Reputation compounds.** Every ethical interaction builds trust capital that pays dividends for years.

4. **Transparency is a competitive advantage.** In an industry with many bad actors, being honest differentiates you immediately.

5. **Your gut knows.** That uncomfortable feeling is data. Listen to it.

## Framework for Future Ethical Decisions

Based on this experience, I developed a five-step framework for navigating ethical dilemmas:

1. **Identify the information gap.** Does the seller understand what you understand about value, options, and market conditions?

2. **Apply the three tests.** Newspaper test, grandmother test, long-term reputation test.

3. **Calculate the true cost.** Factor in reputation damage, legal exposure, referral loss, and psychological impact—not just immediate profit.

4. **Consider the third option.** Beyond "exploit" or "walk away," there's usually a path that serves everyone reasonably well.

5. **Document your reasoning.** Write down why you made the decision you made. This creates accountability and helps refine your ethical compass over time.

## Common Mistakes in Ethical Decision-Making

1. **Rationalizing exploitation as "business"** - Profit motive doesn't excuse predatory behavior
2. **Ignoring gut discomfort** - That feeling exists for a reason
3. **Thinking reputation damage is invisible** - Unhappy sellers talk, post reviews, and file complaints
4. **Undervaluing referral potential** - Ethical deals generate exponentially more long-term business
5. **Binary thinking** - Assuming you must either maximize profit or walk away
6. **Discounting legal exposure** - Predatory practices invite lawsuits and regulatory attention
7. **Focusing only on this deal** - Every transaction shapes your identity as a business professional
8. **Believing sellers won't discover the truth** - They often do, and consequences follow
