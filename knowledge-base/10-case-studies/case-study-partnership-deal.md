# Case Study: How Two Wholesalers Split $17,000 on Their First Partnership Deal

## Quick Reference

- **Partners**: Marcus Chen (marketing specialist) and Sarah Rodriguez (negotiation and disposition expert)
- **Property**: 3BR/2BA single-family home, 1,450 sq ft in Memphis, TN
- **Seller Situation**: Out-of-state heir who inherited the property
- **Contract Price**: $95,000
- **Assignment Fee**: $17,000
- **Profit Split**: 50/50 ($8,500 each)
- **Timeline**: 23 days from lead to closing
- **Key Success Factor**: Complementary skills eliminated individual weaknesses
- **Partnership Structure**: Informal JV with written agreement
- **Communication Method**: Daily check-ins via Slack, weekly video calls

## The Partners: Background and Context

### Marcus Chen - The Marketing Specialist

Marcus had been wholesaling for 14 months when this deal occurred. His strengths included direct mail marketing, skip tracing, and lead generation. He consistently generated 15-25 motivated seller leads per month through his marketing efforts.

However, Marcus struggled with two critical areas: negotiating with sellers on the phone and finding end buyers quickly. His closing rate on qualified leads was only 8%, and deals often fell apart during the disposition phase because he lacked a robust buyer's list.

**Marcus's Resources:**
- Monthly marketing budget: $3,500
- Skip tracing subscriptions and data tools
- CRM with 2,100 motivated seller leads
- Limited buyer's list (47 contacts)

### Sarah Rodriguez - The Disposition Expert

Sarah had been in real estate for six years, starting as a licensed agent before transitioning to wholesaling. Her superpower was negotiation—both acquiring properties and selling to investors. She maintained a buyer's list of 340 active cash buyers and had closed 31 wholesale deals.

Her weakness? Marketing. Sarah hated cold calling, avoided direct mail because of the upfront costs, and struggled to generate consistent lead flow. She often went weeks between deals, waiting for referrals or driving for dollars opportunities.

**Sarah's Resources:**
- Buyer's list: 340 verified cash buyers
- Strong negotiation skills
- Real estate license (inactive)
- Title company relationships
- Limited marketing budget: $800/month

## How the Partners Found Each Other

Marcus and Sarah met at the Memphis Real Estate Investors Association (REIA) monthly meeting in March. During the networking portion, Marcus mentioned his frustration with disposition. Sarah overheard and introduced herself, explaining her opposite problem.

They exchanged numbers and had coffee two days later. During that conversation, they realized their skills were perfectly complementary:

| Marcus's Strengths | Sarah's Strengths |
|-------------------|-------------------|
| Lead generation | Seller negotiation |
| Marketing systems | Buyer relationships |
| Data and skip tracing | Contract knowledge |
| Upfront capital | Closing coordination |

After their coffee meeting, they agreed to try one deal together before formalizing anything long-term.

## The Partnership Agreement

Before working together, Sarah insisted on a simple written agreement. This one-page document outlined:

1. **Profit Split**: 50/50 on all deals where Marcus sourced the lead and Sarah handled negotiation and disposition
2. **Expense Responsibility**: Marketing costs paid by Marcus; disposition costs (if any) paid by Sarah
3. **Decision Authority**: Both partners must agree on contract price and buyer selection
4. **Timeline**: Agreement covers 90 days, renewable by mutual consent
5. **Confidentiality**: Neither partner shares the other's leads or buyers outside the partnership
6. **Exit Clause**: Either party can exit with 14 days written notice

This simple agreement protected both parties and set clear expectations before any money was involved.

## The Deal: 847 Magnolia Street

### Property Details

- **Address**: 847 Magnolia Street, Memphis, TN 38107
- **Property Type**: Single-family residential
- **Bedrooms/Bathrooms**: 3BR / 2BA
- **Square Footage**: 1,450 sq ft
- **Year Built**: 1972
- **Lot Size**: 0.18 acres
- **Neighborhood**: C+ area, primarily rental properties

### The Seller's Situation

The property belonged to James Patterson, a 58-year-old man living in Portland, Oregon. James inherited the house from his aunt who passed away four months earlier. He had no interest in being a landlord, couldn't afford the required repairs, and wanted the property liquidated quickly to settle the estate.

Key motivators:
- Emotional detachment (never lived in the property)
- Distance (2,000+ miles away)
- Carrying costs ($340/month in taxes, insurance, utilities)
- Repair burden ($30,000-$40,000 estimated)
- Estate settlement pressure

### How Marcus Found the Lead

Marcus pulled a list of inherited properties from the county probate records—properties where the heir's address differed from the property address. He sent a personalized letter that read:

> "Dear James, I understand you recently inherited property at 847 Magnolia Street. Managing a property from out of state can be overwhelming, especially during a difficult time. If you're considering selling, I buy houses in any condition and can close on your timeline. No repairs, no agents, no hassle."

James called Marcus's Google Voice number on Day 3 after receiving the letter.

## Division of Labor

### Marcus's Responsibilities (Days 1-7)

1. **Initial call screening** (Day 1): Qualified James as motivated using the LPMAMA framework
2. **Information gathering**: Collected property details, seller timeline, and motivation level
3. **Skip tracing verification**: Confirmed James was the legal heir
4. **Property research**: Pulled tax records, prior sale history, and neighborhood comps
5. **Handoff to Sarah**: Provided detailed notes via shared Google Drive folder

### Sarah's Responsibilities (Days 7-23)

1. **Negotiation calls** (Days 7-9): Three phone conversations with James
2. **Offer presentation** (Day 9): Made initial offer of $88,000
3. **Counter-negotiation** (Days 9-11): Negotiated to final price of $95,000
4. **Contract execution** (Day 11): Sent DocuSign contract, secured signatures
5. **Earnest money deposit** (Day 12): $2,500 deposited with title company
6. **Buyer marketing** (Days 12-15): Blasted property to buyer's list
7. **Buyer showings** (Days 15-17): Coordinated three investor walkthroughs
8. **Assignment negotiation** (Days 17-18): Negotiated assignment fee
9. **Closing coordination** (Days 18-23): Worked with title company to close

## Negotiation and Decision-Making Process

### The Seller Negotiation (Sarah's Approach)

Sarah's first conversation with James lasted 47 minutes. She didn't mention price once. Instead, she focused on understanding his situation, building rapport, and identifying his true priorities.

James revealed that his primary concern wasn't maximizing price—it was avoiding hassle. He didn't want to coordinate repairs, deal with tenants, or make multiple trips to Memphis. Speed and certainty mattered more than an extra $5,000-$10,000.

Sarah's initial offer of $88,000 was rejected, but James countered at $105,000. Rather than splitting the difference, Sarah used a technique called "anchored concession." She raised her offer by small increments—$91,000, then $93,000—while simultaneously reinforcing the value she provided (no repairs, no contingencies, flexible closing).

Final agreement: $95,000 with a 21-day closing window.

### Partner Decision Point

When Marcus saw the $95,000 contract price, he was concerned. The property sat slightly above their MAO calculation. He called Sarah to discuss.

Sarah explained her reasoning: the repair estimate might be conservative, and their strong buyer demand in that zip code justified the tight margin. Marcus agreed to proceed, but they established a rule: if the highest buyer offer didn't leave at least $12,000 assignment fee, they would renegotiate with the seller or exit using their inspection contingency.

This decision-making framework—setting a minimum threshold before proceeding—prevented emotional decisions later.

## Buyer Procurement

Sarah marketed the property three ways:

1. **Email blast** to her 340-person buyer's list (Day 12)
2. **Text message campaign** to her top 50 most active buyers (Day 12)
3. **Posted in local Facebook investor groups** (Day 13)

Results:
- 23 email responses
- 8 text message replies
- 7 buyers requested property packets
- 3 buyers scheduled walkthroughs
- 2 buyers submitted offers

**Winning buyer**: Rodriguez Properties LLC offered $112,000 cash with a 10-day close. The buyer had purchased from Sarah twice before and had verified proof of funds.

## Communication and Coordination

Marcus and Sarah established a simple communication system:

- **Daily Updates**: Brief Slack messages each evening summarizing progress
- **Weekly Calls**: 30-minute video calls every Monday to review pipeline
- **Shared Folder**: Google Drive folder with all documents, notes, and contracts
- **Decision Protocol**: Any decision over $1,000 required both partners' approval via text

This lightweight system kept both partners informed without creating administrative burden.

## Key Lessons Learned

### Lesson 1: Complementary Skills Beat Solo Effort

Individually, this deal might not have happened. Marcus had the lead but would have struggled to close. Sarah had the buyer but couldn't generate the lead. Together, they captured $17,000 that neither would have earned alone.

### Lesson 2: Written Agreements Prevent Conflicts

Even between friends, written agreements protect relationships. Having the profit split documented eliminated awkwardness when the check arrived.

### Lesson 3: Communication Beats Coordination

Over-communicating felt excessive at first, but daily updates meant no surprises. When issues arose, both partners were informed and could contribute solutions.

### Lesson 4: Set Decision Thresholds in Advance

Agreeing on minimum acceptable outcomes before negotiations prevents emotional decisions. Their $12,000 minimum assignment fee rule kept everyone aligned.

### Lesson 5: Trust But Verify

Sarah provided Marcus access to view (not edit) all contracts and communications. This transparency built trust without requiring constant oversight.

## Partnership Best Practices

1. **Start with one deal before long-term commitments**—test compatibility first
2. **Document everything in writing**—profit splits, expenses, decision authority
3. **Choose partners with complementary skills**—not people who do what you already do
4. **Establish communication rhythms**—daily updates prevent misunderstandings
5. **Define exit strategies upfront**—know how to end the partnership gracefully
6. **Keep finances transparent**—share expense documentation and verify distributions
7. **Create decision-making frameworks**—agree on how conflicts get resolved
8. **Review performance regularly**—monthly retrospectives improve future deals
9. **Protect your assets**—don't share your buyer's list or lead sources until trust is proven
10. **Scale gradually**—increase deal volume only after systems work consistently

## Common Mistakes in Partnership Deals

1. **Verbal agreements only**: Always document profit splits in writing before working together
2. **Unequal effort without adjusted splits**: If one partner works significantly more, resentment builds
3. **Sharing sensitive assets too early**: Don't hand over your buyer's list to an unproven partner
4. **No exit clause**: Partnerships end—plan for it from the beginning
5. **Assuming alignment on values**: Discuss ethics, timelines, and deal criteria upfront
6. **Poor communication cadence**: Under-communication creates assumptions; over-communication creates burden
7. **No dispute resolution process**: Decide how disagreements get resolved before they occur
8. **Ignoring personality differences**: Working styles matter—discuss expectations early
9. **Combining personal and business relationships**: Set boundaries if partnering with friends
10. **Failing to define roles clearly**: Ambiguous responsibilities create dropped balls and blame
